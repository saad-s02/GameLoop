## 4. Dietary ground rules: what is safe to print about real restaurants

**Halal.** Halal certification in Ontario and Canada is not a single government-regulated system; more than a dozen private certifying bodies operate, the two most commonly cited in the Toronto area being the Halal Monitoring Authority (HMA, headquartered in Scarborough, ON) and the Islamic Food and Nutrition Council of Canada (IFANCC, based in Mississauga, ON), alongside ISNA Canada Halal Certification, the Canadian Halal Food Certifying Agency (CHFCA), and Canada Halal EC (CHEC), which certified one candidate restaurant in this research. A CBC News report noted that while some provinces regulate halal labelling on retail meat, the certifying organizations themselves remain unregulated, so "halal certified" is only as trustworthy as the specific certifier's own process and reputation, not a single government stamp. The safe, honest three-tier language for the app: use "certified halal" only when a named, checkable certifying body is specifically on record for that exact outlet, ideally cross-checked on the certifier's own restaurant directory the way this research did for Paramount Fine Foods Union Station; use "self-described halal" when the restaurant's own marketing states halal sourcing or a no-pork/no-alcohol policy without naming a third-party certifier (Union Chicken, Holy Cow Japanese Steakhouse here); use "halal-friendly" or "halal options available" when only some menu items are described as halal-sourced, or a source uses soft language like "Muslim-friendly" (Byblos Downtown here). Never upgrade tier two or three language to "certified" without a named, checkable certifier.

**Gluten-free.** The safe distinction is between "celiac-safe" (a dedicated gluten-free kitchen, separate fryers, prep surfaces, and utensils, and staff trained on cross-contact, meaning someone with celiac disease can reasonably eat there) and "gluten-free options" (some menu items are made without gluten-containing ingredients, but are prepared in a kitchen that also handles wheat, barley, and rye, so trace cross-contact is possible, and a responsible restaurant will say so itself). Public-health and celiac-advocacy sources note that shared fryers, shared prep surfaces, and airborne flour dust can all transfer gluten into food that is gluten-free by ingredient list alone, and one crowd-sourced study found roughly a third of restaurant dishes labeled gluten-free still tested positive for gluten, with pizza and pasta the highest-risk categories. None of the fifteen candidates researched here is a fully dedicated gluten-free kitchen. Kupfert and Kim (menu advertised as entirely GF and vegan by concept) is the closest to a strong claim; WVRST and Venezolano have specific, sourced good-practice claims (a dedicated fryer, glove changes per order) worth citing narrowly and specifically rather than as a blanket "gluten-free" badge; Real Sports Bar and Grill and Amano Italian Kitchen are GF-options-only, with an explicit "not a dedicated gluten-free facility" caveat found for Real Sports from at least one allergy-tracking source. Ontario does not legally require restaurants to disclose ingredient or allergen information on menus; this research found no Ontario-specific mandatory disclosure law for restaurants (unlike packaged retail food, which falls under federal Health Canada priority-allergen labelling rules), so any gluten-free or allergen claim the app prints should be sourced to that specific restaurant's own public statement, never assumed from the cuisine type.

**Vegetarian and vegan.** The safest distinction is between a restaurant that is entirely vegetarian or vegan by concept, meaning nothing on the menu contains meat, dairy, or eggs and there is no cross-contact category to worry about (Fresh Kitchen and Juice Bar here), versus a restaurant that offers strong vegetarian or vegan substitutions within a broader omnivore menu (Bangkok Buri, Kupfert and Kim as a brand claim, Grand Tacos Taqueria's vegan items). Print "fully vegetarian and vegan menu" only for the former category and "vegetarian and vegan options" for the latter, and never claim a shared-kitchen restaurant is safe for someone with an allergy-level sensitivity to animal-product cross-contact.

**Nut-free.** This was the thinnest category in the whole research pass. None of the fifteen candidate restaurants had a findable, citable nut-allergy policy: no menu-level tree-nut or peanut disclosure, no "nut-free kitchen" claim, and no allergen matrix located for any of them in the time available. This matches a broader pattern surfaced in general search: Toronto restaurant menus rarely publish structured allergen disclaimers at all (one academic source examining Toronto restaurant menus found only about 16 percent carried any allergy disclaimer at all), and where disclaimers exist they are almost always a generic cross-contact disclaimer ("we cannot guarantee our kitchen is free of common allergens, please notify your server") rather than a nut-specific claim. The honest guardrail for the app: do not print a "nut-free" badge for any real restaurant in this candidate list. At most, print the same generic disclaimer language the app already uses and point users to confirm nut safety directly with the venue. This is worth noting explicitly: the app's own existing lib/copy.ts dietaryDisclaimer pattern, "Listed as offering a [need] item. Cross-contact information is unavailable; confirm with venue staff," turns out to already be the right honest default for real-world restaurants too, not just the app's simulated venue. It should be reused as-is rather than replaced with a more confident-sounding claim when real restaurant data is wired in.

## Integration considerations

### (a) The NO_GEOGRAPHY collision

The app's model prompts explicitly forbid naming the real host city or arena. lib/ai/prompts.ts defines NO_GEOGRAPHY: "Never state or imply the real host city or arena. The venue is Harbourview Arena. Do not invent crowd, weather, or locality detail beyond what the provided data states." That constant is spliced into both EXPLANATION_SYSTEM (the plan-explanation prompt) and RECAP_SYSTEM (the Personal Game Memory prompt), so it governs both AI-authored narration surfaces in the app. Separately, lib/copy.ts hardcodes a footer disclaimer: "Game results and plays shown are real, from the NHL's public record. Harbourview Arena, its gates, concessions, and seat map are fictional, simulated for this demo." Wiring in real, named Toronto restaurants (Union Chicken, Paramount Fine Foods, WVRST, and so on) directly contradicts both of these unless the team makes a deliberate product decision. Three options:

**Full real-city pivot.** Rename the venue to Scotiabank Arena, relax NO_GEOGRAPHY to permit naming Toronto and the arena, rewrite the footer disclaimer, and fold these restaurants into venue.json as off-site "stands" reachable via new walking-graph edges from Union Station. This is the highest-fidelity, highest-impact option, but it touches the TDD-protected deterministic core: lib/planning/candidates.ts, schemas.ts, and venueGraph.ts, plus each of their *.test.ts fixture files, since CLAUDE.md requires "tests first, exact fixtures" for lib/planning and lib/games. It also touches lib/ai/prompts.ts and prompts.test.ts (hand-reviewed, never auto-merged per CLAUDE.md), lib/copy.ts, app/how-it-works/page.tsx, app/page.tsx, app/plan/PlanClient.tsx copy, and evals/plan-cases.json. Rough estimate: 12 to 15-plus files touched, most requiring new fixtures under the TDD discipline, plus a DECISIONS.md entry given the architecture-level nature of the change. Not realistically a one-evening job before a July 21 demo.

**Hybrid.** Keep the venue fictional (Harbourview Arena, footer and NO_GEOGRAPHY untouched), but surface these real Toronto restaurants as a new, clearly-labeled "near the arena" panel or card, provenance-tagged the same way the rest of the app already tags data (a SourceBadge-style component already exists, seen in use in components/GameMemoryCard.tsx), without feeding them into the deterministic candidate-generation and pruning algorithm in lib/planning/candidates.ts. This needs one new read-only fixture (for example lib/data/nearby-real-restaurants.json, shaped like this research's candidates.json), one small new UI surface, and a short hand-written copy addition explaining these are real places near the real city the fiction is modeled on, without the AI-authored narration ever naming the arena or city (NO_GEOGRAPHY stays intact for the model prompts; only a hand-written, non-AI UI card would name real restaurants). Rough estimate: 3 to 5 files touched (one new data file, one new component, one copy addition, possibly one new test), no changes to the TDD-protected planning core and no changes to lib/ai/prompts.ts.
